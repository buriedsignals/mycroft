# SIFT × C2PA Integration: Cryptographic Provenance for Fact-Checking

Mycroft performs rigorous SIFT fact-checking, but today that verification trail exists only as ephemeral chat output and scattered vault notes. There's no cryptographic binding between the sources consulted, the verification performed, and the final published article. This means readers and editors must trust the journalist's word that fact-checking occurred—there's no independently verifiable proof. The SIFT × C2PA integration closes this gap by encoding the entire verification process into a tamper-evident chain that anyone can audit.

The integration captures provenance at the moment of source consultation. When `mycroft-fetch` retrieves a source, it records the URL, a SHA-256 content hash, and an access timestamp. These aren't signed individually (which would be computationally expensive for 20+ sources per investigation), but rather referenced as ingredients in the final C2PA manifest. The hash proves "this is exactly what the source said when I checked"—if disputed, anyone can re-fetch the URL, compare hashes, or check archive.org for that timestamp. This lightweight approach gives cryptographic assurance without the overhead of signing every source.

The staged pipeline creates a claim chain as the artifact progresses through SIFT methodology. Each stage—draft capture, source collection, verification, publication—generates a new C2PA claim that cryptographically references the previous one. This means the provenance isn't just "this article was fact-checked," but rather a complete audit trail: when the draft was captured, which sources were consulted in what order, what verdicts were assigned to which claims, and when final publication occurred. If any stage is tampered with, the chain breaks. This granular provenance is particularly valuable for investigative journalism where the methodology matters as much as the conclusion.

The practical outcome is that SIFT-verified articles carry machine-readable, cryptographically signed proof of their verification. Content platforms, news aggregators, and readers with C2PA-aware tools can instantly see: who performed the fact-check, how many sources were consulted, what verdicts were assigned, and whether the signature chain is intact. This transforms fact-checking from an invisible process into a visible credential—giving rigorous journalism a verifiable advantage over unverified content.
